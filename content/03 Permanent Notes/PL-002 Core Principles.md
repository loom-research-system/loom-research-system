# Core Principles
*What Project Loom believes about governance.*
## 1. Governance capacity is a public good.
The ability of institutions to implement, administer, and sustain policy is not merely an administrative concern. It is a shared resource upon which democratic legitimacy, public trust, and effective governance depend. Like clean water or public health infrastructure, governance capacity is non-rivalrous in its benefits and systematically underproduced when left to individual actors. Societies that neglect it pay compounding costs.
## 2. Mechanisms matter as much as outcomes.
The *what* of policy—its goals, its intentions, its promised results—receives enormous attention. The *how*—the institutional pathways, authorities, dependencies, and operational requirements that determine whether a policy succeeds—receives comparatively little. Project Loom treats the mechanisms of governance as objects of study in their own right, not as mere implementation details to be sorted out after the important decisions have been made.
## 3. Transparency is infrastructure for accountability.
Institutional opacity is not a neutral condition. It is a structural advantage for those who already hold power. When the flows of authority, funding, and decision-making are invisible, accountability becomes impossible. Transparency is not a cosmetic value—it is the foundation on which democratic oversight, public participation, and institutional trust are built. It must be designed into governance systems, not retrofitted after the fact.
## 4. Institutions should be understandable by the people they serve.
A governance system that cannot be comprehended by the citizens whose lives it shapes is, in a fundamental sense, failing. Complexity is real and cannot be wished away, but it can be mapped, explained, and made navigable. The burden of understanding should not fall entirely on the public; institutions bear a responsibility to make their own operation legible.
## 5. Institutional transitions must preserve functional capacity.
Change is necessary. Rupture is costly. When governance systems are transformed—whether through reform, restructuring, or reversal—the capacity to govern must be maintained throughout the transition. Destroying institutional capacity faster than replacements can be built is not revolutionary; it is reckless. Project Loom values resilience over disruption and continuity of function over symbolic victory.
## 6. The object of study is mechanisms, not ideology.
Project Loom does not advocate for specific policies, parties, or political programs. It studies the structures through which any policy must travel to become reality. This commitment is not a claim of neutrality—the project holds strong normative values about transparency, accountability, and governance capacity. But the analytical frameworks it develops are designed to be applicable across political contexts and policy domains.
