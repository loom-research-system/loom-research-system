# Capacity Assessment Framework

## Capacity Domains

| Domain | Assessment Question |
|--------|---------------------|
| Policy capacity | Can the institution design an effective intervention? |
| Legal capacity | Can the institution's authority survive challenge? |
| Administrative capacity | Can the institution manage the operational complexity required? |
| Technical capacity | Can the institution's systems support the required functions? |
| Contractor management | Can the institution effectively oversee external vendors? |
| Financial capacity | Are resources available and allocable? |
| Political capacity | Can the institution sustain support for the intervention? |
| Adaptive capacity | Can the institution diagnose failure and restructure its approach? |

## Rating Scale

| Rating | Definition |
|--------|------------|
| Strong | Domain capability clearly present and demonstrated |
| Adequate | Domain capability sufficient for the task |
| Weak | Domain capability present but insufficient |
| Critically Absent | Domain capability not present when required |
| Unknown | Insufficient evidence to assess |

## Key Principle

**Capacity is domain-specific.** An institution may be strong in some domains and critically weak in others. The diagnosis must identify which domains were insufficient for the specific task, not issue a general judgment of "capable" or "incapable."

## Cross-Case Validation

- [[PL-402 Case Study 001|Case #001]]: Department of Education showed strong administrative and technical capacity but weak legal capacity.
- [[PL-403 Case Study 002|Case #002]]: CMS showed strong policy and legal capacity but critically weak technical and contractor management capacity.
- [[PL-404 Case Study 003|Case #003]]: NHTSA showed adequate-to-strong capacity across all relevant domains.
